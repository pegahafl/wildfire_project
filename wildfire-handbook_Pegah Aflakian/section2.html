Different types of fuels influence fire behavior. Flash fuels like dried grass, leaves, and small branches ignite easily and allow fire to spread rapidly, often generating enough heat to ignite heavier fuels such as tree stumps, logs, or the organic matter on the forest floor. These heavier fuels burn more slowly but are harder to extinguish.

Wildfires are classified into six ranks based on their behavior and intensity. These ranks help describe how a fire spreads, its potential danger, and the tactics needed to fight it.

Rank 1

Smouldering ground fire

Characteristics:

  • Fire is smouldering without a flame.
  • Burning is mainly below ground (organic soils, roots, peat).

Firefighting tactics: Use hand tools and water to expose and extinguish fuels.

Rank 2

Low vigour surface fire

Characteristics:

  • Visible flame but limited spread.
  • Low flame length (less than 1 m).
  • Slow-moving fire on the surface fuels (grass, small shrubs).

Firefighting tactics: Can usually be attacked directly by ground crews with hand tools and water.

Rank 3

Moderately vigorous surface fire

  • More vigorous spread with higher flame length (1‚Äì2 m).
  • Surface fuels burn quickly and fire spreads moderately fast.
  • Can start igniting ladder fuels (shrubs, small trees).

Firefighting tactics: Direct attack possible but challenging. Requires water, hose lines, and coordinated crew action.

Firefighting tactics: Usually requires indirect attack methods and aircraft support.

Rank 6

Extreme & aggressive fire behaviour

Characteristics:

  • Extreme fire behavior with crown fire (fire spreads through tree canopies).
  • Flame lengths exceed 3 m, very fast spread.
  • Produces its own weather (strong convection, spotting, fire whirls).

Firefighting tactics: Direct control impossible. Only indirect strategies and large-scale suppression efforts are used.
